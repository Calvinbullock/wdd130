Sustanible Textiles

So in this article, I will be talking about how to get the most out of the clothing you buy. The first step here is to get durable and high quality, you don't have to buy it new. Thrifting is a very good way to find quality clothing for the cheap. Thrifting keeps clothes out of landfills and helps reduce the strain and pollution of making new clothes. Also looking for deals is another great way to get quality clothes. here are some images of textile items I have had for varying amounts of time. The first is my bag, I have had this bag for 10+ years and it's still in fairly good shape. The bottom is starting to wear out but I hope to find a way to reaper that and help this bag live on. The next is my jacket this jacket and the hoodie next to it are my two main outerwear and I have had both for several years. I have sowed the jacket up a few times myself, and the windbreaker is still in great condition even with a bit of wear. Learning how to repair items dramatically helps to decrease your consumption and waste. Here are some statistics front the U.S government on textiles. The recycling rate for all textiles was 14.7 percent in 2018, with 2.5 million tons recycled. Within this figure, EPA estimated that the recycling rate for textiles in clothing and footwear was 13 percent based on information from the American Textile Recycling Service. The rate for items such as sheets and pillowcases was 15.8 percent in 2018. The total amount of textiles in MSW combusted in 2018 was 3.2 million tons. This was 9.3 percent of MSW combusted with energy recovery. Landfills received 11.3 million tons of MSW textiles in 2018. This was 7.7 percent of all MSW landfilled3. We can reduce what we buy only to what we need and reduce what we trough away if we learn a few simple skills of repair and learn to treasure our textiles and the stories they carry instead of giving into the consumer culture pushing us to the new and never enough mentality
